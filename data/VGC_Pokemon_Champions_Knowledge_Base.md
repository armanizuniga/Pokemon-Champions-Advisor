## INTRODUCTION

### VGC Format Rules — Pokémon Champions Edition

This guide is specifically for **Pokémon Champions**, a battle-focused game released April 2026 for Nintendo Switch and Mobile. Only a curated set of Pokémon are available — no legendaries, no mythicals. The roster spans Generations 1–9 and includes many Pokémon with Mega Evolution forms.

**Rules that always apply:**
- Double battles: 2v2 at all times
- Bring 6, pick 4 (Team Preview)
- Winner = last trainer with Pokémon standing
- Species Clause: only one of each species per team
- **No IVs** — Pokémon Champions removes individual values entirely; stat customization comes entirely from EVs and Natures
- **No legendaries or mythicals** — the available Pokémon pool is a fixed, curated roster
- **Mega Evolution is available** — many Pokémon have Mega forms accessible via Mega Stones held in battle

### Stats Fundamentals

- Six core stats: HP, Attack, Defense, Special Attack, Special Defense, Speed
- **Base Stats**: fixed per species (1–255); the largest determinant of how a Pokémon performs
- **No IVs in Pokémon Champions** — individual values do not exist in this game
- **EVs**: 510 total, max 252 per stat; the primary layer of stat customization. At Level 50, every 8 EVs = 1 stat point (after the first 4)
- **Natures**: +10% to one stat, -10% to another (25 total; some are neutral). Applied after Base Stats and EVs
- **STAB**: Same-Type Attacking Bonus = 1.5x damage when a Pokémon uses a move matching one of its types
- **Type chart**: 18 types; interactions produce 2x (super effective), 0.5x (not very effective), or 0x (immune) damage modifiers
- **Stat boosts/drops**: +1 to +6, -1 to -6 stages; multiplier uses 2/2 base fraction (e.g., +2 = 4/2 = 2x); resets on switch
- **Damage rolls**: each hit can be one of 16 values between a min and max roll — slight variance means a KO is never always guaranteed

## BATTLING

### Before the Battle

**Analyzing Your Opponent's Team**
- Identify their overall strategy before thinking about your own Pokémon
- Look for obvious synergistic combinations: ability-based combos, Fake Out + strong attacker, Speed control + attacker, Redirection + setup
- Assess their overall speed profile and how they control turn order
- Identify any Pokémon with "1v4" potential — one Pokémon capable of threatening your entire lineup

**Team Preview**
- Goal: find a strong lead, pick the best back two, and make sure your four can answer every Pokémon on their side
- Eliminate Pokémon that don't contribute: bad offensive/defensive typing, no relevant output, wrong speed dynamic, part of a combo you can't split
- Identify Pokémon you *must* bring: your main attackers, answers to their key threats
- Your lead should have a clear purpose: setting speed control, applying offensive pressure, or playing defensively
- Your back two should be able to rescue a bad lead match-up
- After each game in a longer set, use revealed information (moves, items, spreads) to adjust your next selection

**Game Plan**
- A game plan = a general approach to how you intend to win the battle
- Start from your team's primary strengths, then modify based on the opponent's team
- Identify your key win conditions: Pokémon to protect, Pokémon to eliminate quickly
- Look for weaknesses in your opponent's team: typing holes, speed reliance, lack of damage output
- Think about battle pace: who has offensive pressure early vs. late? What happens when Tailwind or Trick Room expires?
- Plan turn 1 in your head; have a "safe" play that works against multiple opponent leads
- Game plans are adaptive — improvise as the battle evolves

### General Battling Principles

**Pressure (Action/Reaction)**
- Both players move simultaneously — you can never fully know what will happen in a turn
- **Proactive actions**: deal damage, take KOs, set speed control, apply status conditions
- **Reactive actions**: always in response to a threat — Protect, switch, heal
- **Pressure** = the threat of proactive action one Pokémon applies to another
- Ask yourself: Which Pokémon moves first? Who threatens KOs this turn? What does next turn look like if everyone does their primary action?
- Pressure is your guide to your opponent's thought process — what are they most afraid of losing?

**Predictions**
- Two kinds: (1) anticipating all possible opponent move combinations; (2) calling one specific play
- Don't over-predict — being wrong at the wrong time can cost the game
- Metagame knowledge is a prerequisite: know common items, movesets, and speed tiers for popular Pokémon
- When to go for a specific prediction:
  - *Winning*: only if getting it right wins the game, and being wrong doesn't immediately lose it
  - *Neutral*: if getting it right swings the battle significantly in your favor
  - *Losing*: when you have no safe play, or you're going to lose if you don't call it correctly

**Protect**
- One of the most important moves in VGC doubles; nearly every Pokémon should carry it unless running a Choice item
- Uses: avoid KOs, dodge Fake Out turn 1, reposition safely, stall Trick Room / Tailwind / weather / terrain
- Drawbacks: gives opponent a free turn to switch, set up, or double-target your other Pokémon
- Consecutive Protect odds: only 1/3 — extremely risky; only attempt as an absolute last resort
- Learn which Pokémon commonly carry Protect vs. which rarely do
- Forcing your opponent to Protect is a valid strategy — it denies their move for a turn and makes the following turn harder for them
- Always ask: What do I do the turn *after* I Protect?

**Switching**
- Reasons to switch: survive a threatening attack, improve positioning for next turn, exploit field conditions, save a key Pokémon for later
- Risks: opponent does something unexpected; switch-in takes too much damage; switch-in doesn't improve your position; no damage dealt means no progress toward winning
- Switch-ins are fully exposed to attacks, including secondary effects like crits, burns, and freezes
- Weigh the future payoff of a switch carefully against the cost of losing offensive momentum

**Specific Concepts**
- **1 HP is infinitely more than 0 HP**: a low-HP Pokémon is still threatening — it can still Protect, apply pressure, or absorb an attack. Don't throw it away carelessly
- **Battling against Trick Room**: stall it out with Protect or switching, prevent it from going up with Taunt or Fake Out, or simply wait it out with naturally faster Pokémon
- **How to analyze a battle**: focus on the quality of your decisions, not just the result. Ask: Was my logic sound? What was the best play available to me? What would I do differently?

## TEAMBUILDING

### Foundational Philosophy
- **Intent**: every team starts with an idea — a Pokémon, a strategy, or a mechanic you want to build around
- **You're in charge**: take outside opinions as input, not instruction. Know *why* every Pokémon is on your team
- **Context matters**: a Pokémon's strength is always relative to the current metagame and the specific matchup

### What Makes a Pokémon Good
- Must have **field presence**: the ability to impact the game just by being on the field — through damage output or meaningful support
- Must be **consistent**: effective even when the opponent knows your moveset and item
- Must **challenge the opponent**: force them to respond; ideally force multiple types of responses
- Must **enable teammates**: helps the rest of the team succeed, not just itself
- Good does not equal high usage — uncommon Pokémon can be excellent
- Be cautious of Pokémon that: need a setup move to deal meaningful damage, require a specific field state to function, offer no safe or consistent plays, or have narrow move coverage

---

### Concrete Building Blocks

**Typing**
- Offensive typing: how much of the format can you hit for neutral or super-effective damage?
- Defensive typing: how many common threats can you resist, be immune to, or switch into?
- Avoid stacking the same type weaknesses across your team

**Utility Moves**
- Almost every Pokémon in VGC doubles runs 1–3 non-damaging moves
- Standard moveset template: Protect / STAB 1 / STAB 2 or Coverage / Utility or Coverage
- Priority utility moves for nearly every team:
  - **Protect** — nearly universal; one of the most important moves in doubles
  - **Speed control** (Trick Room, Tailwind, Icy Wind, Electroweb) — controls turn order
  - **Redirection** (Follow Me, Rage Powder) — forces all non-spread moves to target the user, fully protecting the partner for a turn
  - **Fake Out** — first-turn guaranteed flinch; flexible for offensive or defensive use at any stage of the game
  - **Taunt** — shuts down support moves, Protect, Trick Room, sleep, and recovery
- Situational utility:
  - Status: Sleep > Burn > Paralysis >>> Poison; Freeze is best but uncontrollable as a secondary effect
  - Screens: Reflect / Light Screen / Aurora Veil — reduce damage to 2/3 in doubles for 5–8 turns; committal but powerful
  - Stat drops: Icy Wind, Eerie Impulse, Breaking Swipe, Fake Tears
  - Setup moves: Swords Dance, Dragon Dance, Nasty Plot — high risk / high reward; usually needs support (Fake Out, Follow Me) to set up safely
  - Recovery: best on naturally bulky Pokémon that take 40% or less from neutral attacks
  - Anti-setup: Haze removes all stat changes; Roar / Dragon Tail force switches

**Speed Control**
- Moving first = enormous advantage (KO your opponent before they attack = best defense is a strong offense)
- Speed changes are **dynamic** — calculated mid-turn, not just at turn start
- Types:
  - **Tailwind**: doubles your team's Speed for 4 turns (effective 3 after setup); best with fast or Prankster setters like Whimsicott or Talonflame
  - **Trick Room**: reverses Speed order for 5 turns; strongest form of Speed control when properly supported; needs Fake Out or Follow Me to set up safely; naturally counters opposing Tailwind
  - **Speed drops**: Icy Wind, Electroweb, Bulldoze — less committal, but reset when the target switches out
  - **Speed boosts**: Dragon Dance, Quiver Dance, Choice Scarf, Swift Swim / Chlorophyll — more durable advantage than drops
  - **Priority moves**: bypass all Speed mechanics; lower power but invaluable for picking off weakened opponents
  - **Natural Speed**: being the fastest Pokémon when no field effect is active — crucial for turn 1 and after Tailwind / Trick Room expires
- Rule of thumb: have at least **two forms of Speed control** on every team

**Items (Pokémon Champions Legal Items)**

Hold Items:
Black Belt, Black Glasses, Bright Powder, Charcoal, Choice Scarf, Dragon Fang, Fairy Feather, Focus Band, Focus Sash, Hard Stone, King's Rock, Leftovers, Light Ball (Pikachu only), Magnet, Mental Herb, Metal Coat, Miracle Seed, Mystic Water, Never-Melt Ice, Poison Barb, Quick Claw, Scope Lens, Sharp Beak, Shell Bell, Silk Scarf, Silver Powder, Soft Sand, Spell Tag, Twisted Spoon, White Herb

Key item notes:
- **Choice Scarf**: +1.5x Speed, locked into one move — strong opener but punishable on a switch
- **Focus Sash**: survive any one-hit KO from full HP (once) — great on fast, frail Pokémon
- **Focus Band**: may endure a KO with 1 HP (chance-based) — less reliable than Sash
- **Leftovers**: passive HP recovery every turn — best on bulky Pokémon that stay on the field
- **Shell Bell**: restores HP proportional to damage dealt — rewards offensive momentum
- **White Herb**: restores any lowered stats once — best paired with a stat-drop move or to recover from Intimidate
- **Mental Herb**: cures move-locking effects (Taunt, Encore, etc.) once
- **Scope Lens**: boosts critical-hit rate
- **Type-boosting items** (Charcoal, Mystic Water, Magnet, etc.): +~20% to moves of that type

Berries:
- **Lum Berry**: cures any status condition once — strong universal defensive choice
- **Sitrus Berry**: restores some HP automatically when triggered — good on bulky Pokémon
- **Oran Berry**: restores 10 HP — weaker version of Sitrus
- **Chesto Berry**: cures Sleep once
- **Resistance Berries** (Occa, Passho, Yache, Chople, etc.): halve damage from a specific super-effective type once
- **Status cure berries** (Cheri, Pecha, Rawst, Aspear): cure one specific status — usually outclassed by Lum Berry

Mega Stones: each Mega Stone enables a specific Pokémon's Mega Evolution for the duration of battle. Every Mega Stone in the game corresponds to one of the available Mega Evolution Pokémon in the roster.

NOTE: **Flame Orb and Toxic Orb are not in the game** — abilities like Guts, Marvel Scale, and Poison Heal are significantly harder to activate as a result.

**Protect (Teambuilding)**
- Protect is the most-used move in doubles for good reason
- Almost every Pokémon should have it unless running a Choice item
- Factor Protect into your build before finalizing the fourth moveslot

**Trick Room**
- Reverses Speed order for 5 turns — slowest Pokémon move first
- Requires: a bulky setter, slow and powerful sweepers, setup support (Fake Out, Follow Me)
- Countered by: Taunt, Fake Out, fast attackers that KO the setter, or outlasting the 5-turn duration

### Abstract Building Blocks

**Synergy**
- How well your Pokémon cover each other's weaknesses and enable each other's strengths
- **Offensive synergy**: Pokémon work together to take KOs — complementary typing, overwhelming damage, covering each other's blind spots
- **Defensive synergy**: Pokémon can switch in safely for each other based on typing and bulk
- **Anti-synergy**: when Pokémon interfere with each other (e.g., a Misty Terrain setter blocking a Spore user's sleep move)
- Classic defensive cores: Fire/Water/Grass (FWG); Dragon/Fairy/Steel (Fantasy Core)

**Cores and Modes**
- **Core**: 2–3 Pokémon whose synergy is so strong it defines how the team plays; provides direction for the remaining slots
- **Teambuilding Mode**: 2 Pokémon that work explicitly together but are somewhat separate from the rest of the team (e.g., a Trick Room mode on an otherwise fast team)
- **Battling Mode**: a set of 4 Pokémon you habitually bring to certain matchup scenarios — discovered through practice more than theory
- Modes add flexibility but can restrict team preview options; be careful not to let a mode make your team easy to read

**Archetypes**
- An archetype = a popular, optimized core with defined strengths, weaknesses, and team goals
- Archetypes define the metagame — know them so you can prepare for them
- Don't feel locked into an archetype; use one as a starting point, then optimize around it

**Metagame**
- The metagame = which Pokémon and strategies are currently popular
- You don't need to know every Pokémon — knowing the most-used 30–50 is usually enough
- Metagames shift after notable tournament performances
- Use metagame knowledge to anticipate what you'll face — not as a strict blueprint for what you must use

**Consistency**
- Consistent teams reduce exposure to bad luck
- Prefer: high-accuracy moves, strategies that don't rely on one specific field state, Pokémon that are effective even when fully revealed
- Your team determines your luck — more attacks = more chances at secondary effects; fewer risky plays = fewer variance swings

**Team Playstyle and Pace**
- **Offensive / fast teams**: win quickly, rely on Tailwind and high damage output across many turns
- **Trick Room teams**: slow early, explosive mid-game; danger after Trick Room expires
- **Bulky offense**: switch-heavy, grinds out positional advantages
- Know whether your team is early-game or late-game favored, and plan your endgame accordingly

**Surprise Factor**
- Unexpected moves, items, or Pokémon can steal games in best-of-one battles where the opponent has no information and no time to adapt
- High surprise value fades as more people learn your set — balance surprise with reliability
- Don't sacrifice a Pokémon's core function just for novelty

**Accuracy vs. Power**
- Higher power: better expected damage output, but more variance
- Higher accuracy: more consistent, especially important when a KO is not guaranteed
- Consider: how often does missing cost you the game vs. how much does extra power help you secure it?

### Putting It All Together

**EV Spreads**
- Simple spreads: max a key offensive stat, max HP or a defensive stat, remaining EVs in Speed to hit a relevant threshold
- Complex spreads: built around specific damage benchmarks (survive X's attack at Y% HP, OHKO Z with this move), outspeeding specific Pokémon, or avoiding KO thresholds
- Use a damage calculator to test and verify benchmarks before finalizing

**Common Teambuilding Pitfalls**
- **Sixth Pokémon Syndrome**: stuck on the last slot — often signals the first five aren't working together cleanly enough
- **Island Pokémon**: a Pokémon with no meaningful synergy with any teammate; gets isolated and does nothing useful
- **How to beat a Pokémon**: never rely on a single answer; think about which combinations on your team deal with a threat together
- **When to move on from a team**: if the same matchups keep beating you after multiple adjustments, it may be time to rebuild
- **Keep an open mind**: don't dismiss unorthodox ideas before testing them

## ABILITIES

Abilities are passive attributes that activate automatically. Each Pokémon has 2–3 abilities including one Hidden Ability. You can switch abilities freely using **Victory Points (VP)** — regular abilities cost 0 VP, Hidden Abilities cost 500 VP. Pokémon that Mega Evolve gain a new ability for the duration of their Mega Evolution.

### New Abilities in Pokémon Champions (not in previous games)

- **Mega Sol** — Uses moves as if harsh sunlight is active regardless of actual weather
- **Dragonize** — Converts all Normal-type moves to Dragon-type and boosts their power by 20%
- **Piercing Drill** — Contact moves bypass Protect/Detect and deal 1/4 damage through it (all other defensive effects still trigger)
- **Spicy Spray** — Burns attackers that make contact with the user

### Major Status Conditions (only one at a time per Pokémon)

|---|---|
| **Poisoned** | 1/8 max HP damage at end of each turn |
| **Badly Poisoned** | Starts at 1/16, increases by 1/16 each turn; resets to 1/16 on switch-out and return |

| **Paralyzed** | **12.5%** chance of full immobility per turn + Speed halved (Champions change from 25%) |

## TERRAINS

All terrains last **5 turns** and only affect **grounded Pokémon** (Flying-types and Levitate users are unaffected unless grounded).

Note: Terrain-setting abilities (like Rillaboom's Grassy Surge, Indeedee's Psychic Surge) are NOT currently in Pokémon Champions. Terrain must be set manually via moves.

| Terrain | Effect on Grounded Pokémon |
|---|---|
| **Electric Terrain** | Electric moves +30%; Prevents Sleep; Surge Surfer doubles Speed; boosts Rising Voltage |
| **Psychic Terrain** | Psychic moves +30%; **Blocks all priority moves**; boosts Expanding Force |
| **Grassy Terrain** | Grass moves +30%; Heals 1/16 max HP per turn; weakens Earthquake and Bulldoze; Grassy Glide has priority |
| **Misty Terrain** | Halves Dragon-type move power; **Prevents all major status conditions and confusion** |

**Key competitive notes:**
- **Misty Terrain** is the dominant defensive terrain in doubles — shuts down sleep, burn, paralysis, etc. for ALL grounded Pokémon on both sides
- **Psychic Terrain** is a hard counter to Fake Out and all other priority moves against grounded Pokémon
- Terrains affect both sides of the field equally
- Terrain can be overwritten or removed by moves like Defog, Rapid Spin, and Steel Roller

## SWITCHING IN BATTLE

Key rules for Pokémon Champions:

- Switching out **resets stat boosts/drops** for the switched-out Pokémon
- Switching out **does not cure major status conditions** (unless Natural Cure ability)
- Switching out **does cure hidden status conditions** (confusion, infatuation, etc.)
- **Badly Poisoned** damage counter resets when the Pokémon switches out and back in
- The incoming Pokémon is fully exposed to attacks — no protection
- Entry hazards (Stealth Rock, Spikes, Toxic Spikes) activate on every switch-in
- **Shadow Tag** (Mega Gengar) prevents the opponent from switching
- Dragon Tail, Circle Throw, Roar, and Whirlwind force switches (also resets stat changes)
